#### Research methodology

The WoM-BaM project is divided into three major objectives with three work packages each.

#### Obj. 1 - MiniGrid memory maze as testbench for internal representations

We create a testbench to a) understand why SOTA algorithms struggle to properly navigate in large environments and to b) compare these models with our own developments. This testbench is a modified version of the so-called Memory Maze - a spatial navigation task in a complex and scalable environment. Here, we deliberately step back from sensory complexity and instead focus on testing internal representations of environments with arbitrary sizes and additional dvnamics.

#### WP1.1 Implementing the MiniGrid Memory maze

**Problem:** A standardized testbench is needed to isolate and systematically probe the representational limits of SOTA agents as a function of environmental complexity. Three-dimensional input of the original memory maze increases computational demand without contributing to the core question. Approach: We will implement the MiniGrid Memory maze, a new reinforcement learning environment based on the MiniGrid library and Gymnasium interface. It will replicate the long-term memory and navigation challenges of the original Memory Maze but with simpler, low-dimensional observations to increase performance and eliminate biases from model-specific vision encoders. We will systematically increase environmental complexity by varying maze size, topological intricacy, the number of aliased observations, and adding context-dependent challenges (e.g., doors whose state depends on past actions of agent or others) that require long-term memory. Evaluation: Online mode: First, we will evaluate the performance of SOTA algorithms, such as DreamerV34, R2I5, as well as those that have to our knowledge not been tested on the Memory Maze, like TransDreamer<sup>6,7</sup> and a custom Dreamer version with qLSTM<sup>8,9</sup> instead of a gated recurrent unit (GRU) (relevant for WP1.3), in a reward-seeking mode to establish under which conditions these algorithms fail. Offline mode: We will create an offline dataset, in which the agent is instructed to freely explore the environment, similar to1. The offline dataset ensures fair comparison as every algorithm receives the same training data. The vanilla CSCG version will also be trained on the offline dataset 10 and serve as reference. Contribution: This work package will deliver a novel, configurable, and open-source testbench designed to rigorously and systematically evaluate the representational capabilities of world-model agents.

### WP1.2 Comparing models in MiniGrid Memory maze

Problem: Standard performance metrics like reward are insufficient to explain why state-of-the-art world models fail in large, complex environments. A deeper analysis is required to characterize and compare the internal representational structures of different agents to determine if they are learning a flexible, map-like world model or simply memorizing sequential paths. Approach: We will employ a suite of analytical tools from computational neuroscience to conduct an in-depth comparative analysis of the models' internal representations and emergent behaviors. Evaluation: To understand single-unit activity, we will train decoding models, such as generalized linear models, to analyze learned kernels and distinguish between sparse, selective codes and dense, multiplexed codes11. At the population level, we will combine simple and advanced measures to study the structure and disentanglement of the neural activity manifold. For instance, we will use population vector correlations to test for the separation of different contexts 2 and employ a joint autoencoder<sup>13</sup> to estimate the dimensionality of the representation. Furthermore, we will use decoding approaches1 to determine the complexity of the readout network required for accurate decoding. Additionally, for data from the online mode, we will combine internal representations with an in-depth analysis of the agent's navigation strategies, specifically its ability to take novel shortcuts versus relying on path-following, in line with our previous work 14,15. Contribution: This work will provide a deeper understanding of model failure modes beyond simple reward metrics and establishes the empirical basis for developing agents with more robust and parsimonious world models in subsequent WPs.

#### Obj. 2 - Developing a brain-inspired hybrid planning architecture

Our goal is to bridge the gap between parsimonious representations of CSCG and the scalability and expressive power of modern deep learning algorithms. Our preliminary work successfully reformulated CSCG as a differentiable module within TensorFlow, minimizing the negative log-likelihood of observation and action sequences<sup>16</sup>. This crucial first step allows us to treat the algorithm as a layer in a larger network and train it end-to-end with a compound loss via gradient descent<sup>17</sup>. Building on this foundation, we will progressively enhance its canabilities in three distinct work packages.

# WP2.1 Scalable and Dynamic CSCG through Learnable Components

Problem: The current CSCG formulation requires pre-allocating a sufficient number of clones per unique observation, making it computationally inefficient in large and diverse environments10,18. To prevent most clones remain unused, we aim to recruit clones demand19 Approach: We will implement two complementary strategies for on-demand

recruitment. First, we will replace the fixed emission matrix with a learnable, neural emission layer<sup>17</sup>. By introducing competition<sup>12,20</sup> among the layer's weights (e.g., via a softmax function or a sparsity-inducing regularizer), we will enable the model to dynamically re-attribute observations to a smaller pool of clones. Second, we will implement an adaptive masking mechanism that computationally disconnects inactive clones from the gradient flow, ensuring they do not slow down computation. Evaluation: The profromance of our dynamic neuralized CSCG will be compared with its static counterpart on synthetic sequence data, isolating computational performance. We will systematically vary the number of required clones and measure computational efficiency and model accuracy. We hypothesize that the dynamic CSCG will show significantly better scaling in large environments. Contribution: This work will produce a scalable and resource-efficient CSCG variant. This is a foundational contribution, enabling the application of CSCG-like structured models to problems of a scale previously out of reach.

#### WP2.2: Hybrid Architectures for Flexible Temporal Integration

Problem: A purely state-based model like CSCG struggles to represent unstructured temporal information. For example, the time from a cue to an event cannot be represented in its current form. A concrete example of this is the temporal delay between the tone and the footshock in the 11-task experiment that will be analyzed in Obj. 3. A standard RNN excels at such sequential tasks but, as we hypothesize, fails to build a stable, structured map of the environment. Approach: Inspired by dual-process theory from psychology, we will design a hybrid architecture. A standard RNN, such as LSTM or GRU, will act as an intuitive System I, processing the raw observation sequence. In parallel, the well-structured, differentiable CSCG module will act as a deliberate System 2. The core innovation is a learnable gating network. That dynamically arbitrates between the two systems, assigning a reliability weight to each input item based on the current context. The

final prediction is a weighted combination, allowing the agent to leverage the powerful, context-integrating capabilities of the RNN when needed, while falling back on the robust, structured representation of the CSCG for stable planning. Evaluation: We will design specific tasks within the offline version of the MiniGrid Memory Maze to prove the necessity of this hybrid design. For example, a task where a final door only opens after the agent has pushed a moving button x times. We will compare internal representations of our hybrid network against pure CSCGs and RNNs. Contribution: This will result in a novel, end-to-end trainable neural-symbolic hybrid model<sup>34</sup>, combining the strengths of structured, symbolic reasoning with the flexibility of deep sequence models.

# WP2.3: Graph-Augmented Planning in Model-Based RL

Problem: The standard Dreamer agent plans by imagining trajectories - a process of computationally expensive latent rollouts4,25. This is inefficient for long-horizon, goal-directed navigation. Approach: We will create a Graph-Augmented Actor-Critic26 by leveraging the dual nature of our hybrid world model (Fig. 2), a) Architectural Integration: The CSCG-RNN hybrid from WP 2.2 will replace Dreamer's standard GRU, serving as the agent's world model. b) Dual-Mode Planning with Learned Arbitration: The agent's actor-critic will be enhanced to operate in two modes. The default mode involves learning a policy through standard latent imagination. The key innovation is a second mode, direct graph-based planning, which is triggered autonomously by a learnable meta-controller. c) Mechanism: The decision to switch from reactive, short-horizon planning to deliberative, graph-based planning is based on the agent's own estimate of task difficulty. A meta-controller will monitor the value function learned by the critic. When the critic's confidence in reaching a given goal falls below a learned threshold (indicating high uncertainty, typical for distant or difficult goals), the meta-controller triggers the graph-planning mode. Once triggered, the system identifies the current and goal locations as nodes on the CSCG's explicit graph and executes a classical search algorithm (e.g., A\*) to find the optimal path. This optimal plan from the graph search will not replace the actor-critic but will powerfully guide it. The shortest path provides an improved heuristic for the critic's value function, and the sequence of optimal actions provides a strong learning signal for the actor's policy. This allows the agent to autonomously decide when to stop and think by consulting its internal map, enabling it to learn complex policies far

more sample-efficiently than by relying on latent rollouts alone. The system will be trained with a composite test function where the non-differentiable graph search outputs are used as highly informative training targets for the differentiable actor and critic networks. Evaluation: The CSCG-RNN network will be evaluated on the online MiniGrid Memory Maze, benchmarked against SOTA across three key axes: a) Sample Efficiency, b) Planning Efficiency, c) Zero-Shot Generalization. To demonstrate robustness on complex sensory inputs, we will evaluate the agent's ability to learn a world model from the existing offline dataset of the original, pixel-based Memory Maze, by adding the Dreamer's vision encoder. Contribution: This work will deliver a new class of RL agent that synergistically combines symbolic planning (efficient, optimal graph search) with sub-symbolic policy learning (flexible, reactive actor-critic). This hybrid approach is expected to drastically improve performance and sample efficiency on tasks requiring long-term memory and planning.

#### Obj. 3 - Testing and explaining hippocampal activity in dynamic environments

This objective closes the outlined NeuroAI loop by directly comparing existing and newly developed algorithms with experimental data. To achieve this, we will leverage a unique, unpublished in-vivo miniscope dataset, recorded from wild-type mice exposed to II different tasks/contexts, including two linear tracks, open fields, elevated plus maze as well as trace fear conditioning, combined with tone associations and movable objects (Fig. 3). This dataset provides an unprecedented opportunity to understand how hippocampal activity flexibly changes in dynamic environments. Recordings are performed on 7 mice with a MiniscopeV4 on pyramidal cells in CAI over 34 days with AAVCAMKILSoma.GCamp, allowing to track

the activity of individual neurons across days. Animals perform every task for around 10 minutes. Previous studies have looked at representational drift over time<sup>27-28</sup>, contextual encoding<sup>30-32</sup>, the effect of emotional states on representations<sup>31</sup>, but the combination of many different tasks with recordings of hundreds to thousands of neurons simultaneously across weeks and contexts makes this dataset unique. Mice are exposed to a T-maze that either forms two linear tracks or allows for a cue-dependent choice, one familiar (optionally with objects) and two novel open fields as well as a trace-fear-conditioning box (Fig. 4). Animals are trained on 18 days within 4 weeks and then again on day 33 and 34. With this dataset we aim to answer fundamental questions about internal representations, such as 1) how do representations emerge, 2) how stable are they over time, both on the single cell and population level, 3) how efficiently is structural information reused (comparing adjacent linear tracks), 4) how is the association and the temporal delay between a tone and a negative emotion (foot shock) encoded, 5) how does negative association affect encoding and place preference in neutral T-maze, where tone is associated with only one linear track, 6) how does reinforcement affect encoding (fear-trace memory is boosted two times), 7) how is negative association un-learned over time and 8) can differences in internal representations explain the strong variability in behavior across animals

# WP 3.1: Preprocessing and Activity Extraction from Miniscope Data

Problem: To perform a meaningful analysis, we must first extract reliable single-neuron activity traces from raw, single-photon calcium imaging data. Approach: We will process the raw video data using user-friendly, memory-efficient and open-source analysis pipelines, such as Minian<sup>14</sup>. Necessary preprocessing steps are motion correction, tracking of individual neurons, and extraction of their corresponding raw calcium traces. Finally, we will deconvolve the calcium signals to obtain an estimate of the underlying neural spiking activity for each cell. Further, we extract animal behavior from video, including simple features such as position and speed, and specific behaviors with the help of pretrained classifiers<sup>153-C</sup> Contribution: This work package will produce a high-quality, curated dataset of neural activity with single unit resolution from the dorsal hippocampal region CA1 across tasks, precisely aligned with the animal's behavior.

Comparable to WP1.2, we will characterize cell activity both on the single-neuron and on the population level. We will use decoding models such as Generalized Linear Models<sup>11</sup>, to determine how the tuning of individual place cells changes across tasks. #### WP3.3: Normative models of hippocampal activity in complex environments.

Problem: Are existing or newly developed world models suitable normative models for hippocampal activity during the 11-task experiment? Approach: We will compare neuronal properties identified in WP3.2 with existing and newly developed world model networks, including RNNs, state-space models, vanilla CSCG and the newly developed CSCG-RNN hybrid. To this aim, we build upon our experience from WP1.1 and build a novel Miniforid environment reflecting the 11-tasks experiment. After training world models on the simulated environment, we will characterize their internal representations similar to WP1.2 and WP3.2. To direct agents we will include rewards similar to those in real experiments, such as positive rewards in

T-maze, chasing food pellets in open fields, curiosity of exploring objects and negative rewards such as foot-shocks that can be "alleviated" upon freezing in the correct temporal window. **Evaluation**: We will evaluate which model architecture best explains hippocampal activity. **Contribution**: This work package culminates the project by establishing a new, biologically-grounded benchmark for world models. It will deliver a definitive, quantitative comparison of which architecture - SOTA models, CSCG, or our novel CSCG-RNN hybrid - best explains hippocampal activity in complex, dynamic environments. Any identified discrepancies will not be failures, but will instead provide a concrete blueprint for the next cycle of NeuroAI innovation, yielding specific, falsifiable hypotheses to guide future development.

#### **Ethics**

This project demonstrates strong ethical compliance by prioritizing human agency, transparency, and accountability in its AI design. A core objective is to create a hybrid architecture with *explicit and interpretable knowledge representations* that can be *inspected and validated before deployment.* This approach moves beyond opaque *black box* models, enabling crucial human oversight and fostering trust, particularly for future applications in safety-critical domains like healthcare and autonomous mobility.

The project's data governance is also robust; it primarily utilizes a unique dataset of hippocampal brain activity from mice, not humans, thereby avoiding direct concerns over personal data privacy. Authorization is formally secured through written consent from the lab of Tom McHugh, both for analysing and publishing. The use of non-human data effectively eliminates the risk of introducing or amplifying societal discrimination and bias.

The research is strongly oriented toward positive societal and environmental outcomes. By aiming to create a new generation of autonomous systems that are more capable, interpretable, and data-efficient, the project contributes to building a foundation for safer and more reliable AI. It also addresses ecological concerns by developing more sample-efficient algorithms promoting sustainability in AI. As the work is focused on understanding the principles of world models in brains and machines, the potential for negative social impacts is minimal. The focus on understanding and mitigating the failure modes of current AI helps ensure that future technological advancements are more robust and ethically aligned.
